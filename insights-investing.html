Investment fundamentals

TFSA, RRSP, RESP, or non-registered — which account should you invest in first?

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
7 min read
Investment planning

One of the most common questions I get from clients — especially those just starting to invest — is: "I have some money to invest. Where should I put it?" The answer depends on your income, your goals, whether you have children, and what your tax situation looks like in retirement. But there is a general priority order that works for most Canadians.

The recommended priority order for most Canadians

1

Employer match (if available) — always first

If your employer matches RRSP contributions, contribute at least enough to capture the full match. This is an immediate 50–100% return on your money before any investment growth.

2

RESP — if you have children under 17

Contribute at least $2,500/year per child to receive the maximum annual Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) of $500. The government is literally giving you free money — this should be a priority before most other investing.

3

TFSA — flexible, powerful, and almost always worth filling next

For most Canadians, the TFSA is the next best home for your money. Tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals make it ideal for almost any goal — whether that's a home, car, emergency fund, or retirement savings.

4

RRSP — especially if you're in a high tax bracket

If you're earning $80,000 or more, RRSP contributions deliver powerful tax refunds that you can reinvest. The higher your income today, the more valuable the RRSP deduction becomes — especially if you expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement.

5

Non-registered — once registered accounts are maximized

Once you've maximized your TFSA and RRSP, non-registered investing is an excellent next step. There are no contribution limits, and capital gains receive preferential tax treatment compared to regular income.

Carrie's practical tip: For most of my clients with children, we prioritize the RESP first (to capture government grants), then split remaining savings between the TFSA and RRSP based on current income. If you're unsure which order is right for your situation, let's walk through it together — the right answer changes based on your income, family situation, and retirement timeline.

TFSA vs RRSP: the key question to ask yourself

The single most important factor in choosing between TFSA and RRSP is: Is your tax rate today higher or lower than it will be in retirement?

  • If your income is high now (and you expect a lower income in retirement) → RRSP wins: the deduction saves you tax at your high rate now; you pay tax at a lower rate later.
  • If your income is moderate now (or you expect a similar or higher income in retirement) → TFSA wins: no tax deduction now, but no tax on withdrawals ever — and it doesn't affect your OAS, GIS, or other income-tested benefits.
  • If you're unsure → do both: split contributions between TFSA and RRSP based on your available room and cash flow.

RESP

RESP explained: how to maximize the government grant for your child's education

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
5 min read

The Registered Education Savings Plan is one of the most underutilized financial tools available to Canadian parents. It offers tax-sheltered growth, government grants, and a flexible structure — yet a surprising number of families either don't have one or aren't contributing enough to capture the full government match.

$500 Maximum annual CESG grant per child when you contribute $2,500/year
$7,200 Lifetime CESG grant available per child (ages 0–17)
20% Government match rate on the first $2,500 contributed per year

How the Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) works

The government contributes 20% of your annual RESP contributions, up to a maximum of $500/year per child (on the first $2,500 you contribute). This grant is automatically deposited into your RESP — no application needed, just contribute. Over 14+ years of contributions, this adds up to a maximum lifetime grant of $7,200 per child.

Low- and middle-income families may also qualify for the Canada Learning Bond (CLB) — up to $2,000 per child — with no contribution required to receive it.

Key RESP rules to know

  • Open early: You can open an RESP for a child from birth. The earlier you start, the more years of compound growth you benefit from.
  • Catch-up contributions: If you miss a year, you can catch up unused CESG room in future years — but only $1,000/year in additional grant is allowed.
  • Lifetime contribution limit: $50,000 per child. Over-contributing is subject to a 1% monthly penalty.
  • Flexible use: If your child doesn't attend post-secondary, you have options — transfer to a sibling's RESP, roll into your RRSP (if you have room), or withdraw your contributions.
  • Who pays tax on withdrawals: The investment growth (Educational Assistance Payments) is taxed in the student's hands — at their typically low tax rate. Your original contributions come back to you tax-free.

Carrie's tip: The $2,500/year target is a sweet spot — it's the amount that maximizes the annual CESG grant without over-contributing. For a child born today, contributing $2,500/year from birth to age 17 would result in approximately $7,200 in government grants plus all the investment growth on top. It's one of the best deals in Canadian personal finance.

Mutual funds

How Sun Life mutual funds work — and how to choose the right ones for your goals

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
6 min read

Mutual funds are the primary investment vehicle Carrie uses with her clients at Sun Life. They offer built-in diversification, professional management, and a range of risk profiles — making them well-suited for investors at all stages of life. But not all mutual funds are the same, and choosing the right ones matters enormously over the long run.

What is a mutual fund?

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and invests it in a diversified portfolio of securities — such as Canadian and global stocks, bonds, real estate, or a combination. Each investor owns units of the fund proportional to their contribution. Instead of picking individual stocks yourself, you benefit from professional portfolio management and instant diversification.

The main types of mutual funds Carrie uses

  • Equity funds — invest primarily in stocks. Higher growth potential over the long term, but more short-term volatility. Best suited for investors with a longer time horizon (10+ years) or higher risk tolerance.
  • Fixed income (bond) funds — invest in government and corporate bonds. More stable returns with lower risk. Well-suited for retirees or conservative investors seeking income and capital preservation.
  • Balanced funds — combine stocks and bonds in a single fund. A popular choice for investors who want a simple, all-in-one portfolio with moderate risk and growth.
  • Money market funds — invest in very short-term, low-risk instruments. Ideal as a parking place for cash or for extremely conservative investors.
  • Target date / lifecycle funds — automatically adjust the asset mix as your retirement date approaches, becoming more conservative over time. A hands-off solution for retirement savers.

How Carrie selects funds for clients

Carrie's fund selection process starts with understanding three things: your investment time horizon, your risk tolerance, and your financial goals. From there, she builds a portfolio across Sun Life's fund lineup that balances growth potential with appropriate protection — and reviews it with you regularly to make sure it stays aligned as your life changes.

The most important thing: The best mutual fund is the one you'll stay invested in through market ups and downs. A portfolio slightly more conservative than optimal — but one you won't panic-sell during a downturn — will outperform a theoretically perfect portfolio that you abandon at the wrong time.

Risk & returns

What is your risk tolerance — and why does it matter for your investments?

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
5 min read

Risk tolerance is one of the most important — and most misunderstood — concepts in personal investing. It's not just about how much volatility you can mathematically afford. It's about how you'll feel and behave when your portfolio drops 25% in a month. Because at some point, it will.

Two types of risk tolerance

  • Financial capacity for risk: How much volatility can your financial situation actually absorb? This depends on your timeline, income stability, emergency fund, and whether you need to draw on the investment in the near future.
  • Emotional tolerance for risk: How would a significant market drop affect your sleep and your decision-making? Would you hold steady, or would you sell everything to stop the pain?

Both matter. Many investors overestimate their emotional risk tolerance during a bull market — then discover their true tolerance during the first serious correction. The 2020 COVID crash took the TSX down 37% in five weeks. How you would have responded to that tells you a lot about where your portfolio should actually be positioned.

Market timing

Why trying to time the market almost always backfires — and what to do instead

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
4 min read

Every week I hear from clients who want to wait until the market "calms down" before investing, or who pulled money out because things felt uncertain. It's completely understandable — but the data is clear: trying to time the market consistently destroys long-term returns.

10 Days: missing the 10 best market days in a decade can cut your returns in half
~80% Of the best market days occur within two weeks of the worst days
$0 Cost of staying invested during every major market crash in history — if you held on

The real cost of being out of the market

Studies consistently show that the best trading days tend to cluster right around the worst trading days. If you exit the market after a crash — hoping to re-enter at the bottom — you're very likely to miss the sharpest recovery days, which do most of the heavy lifting on long-term returns. Missing just the 10 best days in a decade can cut your portfolio's growth roughly in half.

What to do instead: dollar-cost averaging

Rather than trying to pick the perfect moment to invest, dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the strategy that actually works for most investors. You invest a fixed amount at regular intervals — monthly or bi-weekly — regardless of what the market is doing. When prices are high, you buy fewer units. When prices are low, you buy more. Over time, this smooths out the impact of volatility and removes emotion from the equation entirely.

  • Set up an automatic monthly contribution to your TFSA, RRSP, or RESP
  • Invest through market highs and lows without watching the market daily
  • Focus on your personal financial goals — not the daily news cycle
  • Review your portfolio with Carrie once or twice a year to rebalance if needed

The bottom line: Time in the market beats timing the market — every time, over every long-term period studied. The best investment strategy is one you can stick with. Let's build yours together.
