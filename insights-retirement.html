Retirement fundamentals

How much do you really need to retire comfortably in Canada?

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
8 min read
Retirement planning

One of the most common questions I hear from clients is: "How much do I actually need to retire?" The honest answer is: it depends on your lifestyle, your health, where you live, and how long you live. But there are clear frameworks we can use to get to a number that actually makes sense for you.

The "70% Rule" — and why it's not enough

You've probably heard the rule of thumb: you'll need 70% of your pre-retirement income to maintain your lifestyle in retirement. It's a starting point, but it can be dangerously misleading. If you have an active retirement — travel, hobbies, helping grandchildren — you may need closer to 90% or more in the early years. Conversely, if your mortgage is paid off and your lifestyle is modest, 60% might be plenty.

The better approach is to build a retirement budget based on what you actually want your life to look like.

$756K Estimated savings needed for a comfortable retirement (BMO, 2024)
$1,364 Max monthly CPP payment in 2025 (at age 65)
$727 Monthly OAS payment in 2025 (age 65–74)

What government benefits will you receive?

Most Canadians can count on two main government income sources in retirement. It's important to understand what they actually pay — and their limits.

  • Canada Pension Plan (CPP): Based on your lifetime contributions. The maximum in 2025 is approximately $1,364/month at age 65 — but the average is much lower, around $760/month.
  • Old Age Security (OAS): A flat benefit available to most Canadians aged 65+. In 2025, it pays approximately $727/month. High earners (over ~$93,000/year) may have it clawed back.
  • Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS): An additional benefit for lower-income retirees — worth knowing about if your income will be modest.

Combined, CPP and OAS could provide roughly $2,000–$2,100/month — a helpful foundation, but rarely enough on its own for a comfortable retirement.

Carrie's rule of thumb: If your target retirement income is $6,000/month, government benefits might cover $2,000 of that. Your personal savings need to generate the other $4,000 — which requires roughly $1.2–$1.5 million in savings, depending on your investment returns and drawdown strategy.

Building your retirement income plan — step by step

1

Define your retirement lifestyle

What does your ideal retirement look like? Where will you live? Will you travel? Do you want to support your children or grandchildren? Write down your monthly expenses — both essential and discretionary.

2

Estimate your government income

Use the Government of Canada's My Account portal to see your CPP Statement of Contributions. This will give you a realistic estimate of what your CPP benefit will be based on your actual contribution history.

3

Calculate your "savings gap"

Subtract your government income and any pension income from your target monthly income. The remaining amount is what your personal savings (RRSP, TFSA, non-registered) need to generate each month.

4

Project your required savings pool

Using a conservative 4% drawdown rule as a starting point, multiply your annual "savings gap" income need by 25 to estimate the portfolio size required. A financial planner can refine this based on your actual investment mix and expected returns.

5

Build your savings strategy

With a clear target in mind, Carrie will help you set up the right combination of RRSP, TFSA, and investment accounts to reach your goal — with regular reviews to keep you on track.

Don't forget these often-overlooked costs

  • Healthcare costs: Drug plans, dental, vision, and potential long-term care costs increase significantly as you age. Budget for these proactively.
  • Inflation: Even at 2% inflation, your purchasing power drops by roughly 40% over 25 years. Your retirement income plan must account for this.
  • Home maintenance or downsizing: Major repairs, condo fees, or the costs of moving to a smaller home can be significant one-time expenses.
  • Helping family members: Many retirees end up financially supporting children, grandchildren, or aging parents. Factor this into your plan honestly.

The bottom line: There's no single number that works for everyone. The best retirement plan is one that's built specifically around your life. Book a complimentary conversation with Carrie to build your personalized retirement projection.

RRSP Strategy

RRSP vs TFSA: Which should you prioritize in your 40s and 50s?

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
5 min read

I'm often asked: "Should I put my extra savings in my RRSP or my TFSA?" The answer depends on one key factor — your tax rate today vs. your expected tax rate in retirement.

The core difference

Both accounts offer tax-sheltered growth, but they differ in when you pay tax:

  • RRSP: You get a tax deduction now (saving tax at your current marginal rate), but pay tax on every dollar you withdraw in retirement.
  • TFSA: No deduction now, but withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free — and don't affect your eligibility for OAS or GIS.

Simple rule: If your income is high today (and you expect it to be lower in retirement), the RRSP deduction is more valuable. If your income is moderate or you're unsure what taxes will be in retirement, the TFSA is often the safer bet. In most cases — do both.

Why your 40s and 50s are your most important RRSP years

In your peak earning years, you're likely in a higher tax bracket than you'll be in retirement. This is the window when RRSP contributions deliver the most powerful tax savings. A $20,000 RRSP contribution in a 43% tax bracket saves you $8,600 in taxes this year alone.

The TFSA advantage in retirement

TFSA withdrawals don't count as income — meaning they won't trigger OAS clawbacks, affect your GIS eligibility, or inflate your tax bracket. For retirees who want to manage their income strategically, TFSA withdrawals are a powerful tool. Ideally, you want to enter retirement with a healthy balance in both accounts so you can draw from them strategically each year.

When should you start CPP? The answer might surprise you

By Carrie Wang, CEA, CLU®
6 min read

Canada Pension Plan gives you a choice: start as early as age 60 (at a reduced rate), at 65 (the standard), or as late as age 70 (at a significantly higher rate). Which is right for you depends on your health, other income sources, and how long you expect to live.

−36% Reduction if you start CPP at age 60 vs 65
+42% Increase if you delay CPP to age 70 vs 65
~83 Break-even age if you delay from 65 to 70

The case for waiting until 70

If you delay CPP to age 70, you receive 42% more per month than if you took it at 65 — for the rest of your life. If you live past approximately 83, you will receive more in total by waiting. Given that the average Canadian woman today can expect to live to 87, waiting is often the right choice — especially if you have other income sources to draw from in your mid-60s.

When taking CPP early makes sense

There are legitimate reasons to start CPP earlier — including poor health, limited other income, or a strong desire to stop working at 60. The key is making the decision consciously, with a full projection of lifetime income, rather than defaulting to "take it as soon as possible."
